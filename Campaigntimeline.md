## created player onboarding reference
A simple file, with the following contents: 

An Introduction
To whom it may concern,
Congratulations! You’ve made it through the (not very high) bar and gained a seat at the reverent table of D&D. let this document serve as a reference so that your characters - as amazing as they are, can feel like they belong in this strange world of dark magic and ancient history.
Before we kick off, make sure you have seen my D&D server post and the subsequent details in the thread. I will repeat the specific technical details here, but I will assume you already know what you’re getting into.


Building your character
As stated, this campaign will feature heavily in exploring the mysterious world, and investigating its secrets. This is a campaign built on intrigue, lore, and problem solving.
For that reason, martial characters may feel underutilised, especially out of combat. 
Similarly, some spellcaster builds are quite weak at early levels. 
My aim with this campaign is to ensure that nobody’s mechanical chassis gets in the way of their roleplay or storytelling. For that reason, the following mechanical conditions must be met by any character, regardless of background. Again, everything is subject to further discussion if explicitly needed.
Statline
Each character will use point buy for absolute fairness across all PCs. 
Species 
I will accept options in the PHB, and likely, XTGE and TCOA. Options outside of that will need to reasonably match the world lore. For example, warforged do not exist currently in this historical ERA.
Backgrounds
WOTC changed how backgrounds work significantly in 2024 vs 2014, as they now give origin feats. We play with 2014, I’d be happy to supplement your characters with a unique origin feat to make up the difference. 
Choosing a class
Please do not feel pressured to pick a class to “fill a party slot”. I would humbly encourage a wide range of characters and classes with unique abilities, as basic requirements can be met through equipment, which can be purchased or found through adventuring.
For example, healing potions, antitoxins, magical weapons, spell scrolls, etc
Please be aware of this: I am not the game designer for D&D. I will try to aid you as best I can, but I cannot overhaul the class. Some will naturally feel weaker or stronger at certain stages of the game. 
Multiclassing
Multiclassing is not advised, and as of right now, not allowed*. Trying to balance them can cause problems, and I want to ensure no one PC feels out of place in progression. 
Class progression
Though we are starting at level 2, I do intend to end the campaign quite late, well into level 10+. Some classes naturally will dip in power scaling as they progress. 
NPCs and background
I encourage everyone to write unique backgrounds, and if you wish, additional relationships, that tie them into the world at large, as this is a campaign that will be strongly driven by your PCs and what they decide to do with their own future.
However, please note; the explicit role as DM i will serve is to craft and administrate the world at large. I will work with you to interweave your PC into the world, but I cannot promise that everything you lay out for me is fulfilled in the way you expect it to be.
Again, this is a campaign that has elements of cosmic horror. NPCs - if you hand them to me, your DM, you are handing them into the lovecraftian machine. 

Setting and premise
This is still, foundationally, a high fantasy campaign, which means it’s steeped in lore and magic. The following are just some common questions I get asked, that are good to know:
Firearms? Yes, they exist, in a primitive form, at that. However, you will not start with them, and they are not cheap.
Warforged / githyanki / mind flayer / shadar kai race options? The campaign setting at large doesn't involve alternate dimensions or planes. So no, these specific options are not available as your own PC or NPCs.
Magic? Yes! Plenty of magic. As i mentioned, spell scrolls and magic items are available if a specific spellcaster class isn’t selected.
Grit? This campaign will feature some gritty combat and exploration sequences where sustenance, injuries, and plenty of others will impact your character. But as a general setting, it does not fall under a grimdark type campaign.

# 21 / 07 - the lore expansion
- Following the introduction of players, I noticed that the campaign flier had an intriguing hook, but I had yet to hand out some real lore to cling on to:

### background item

- This was the first moment of DM-to-player design prompting. instead of forcing them to play a character specific to my constraints, I would use what they give me to tell my tale.

Think of some nonmagical trinket, heirloom, personal collection, or just any sort of personally relevant object. It should be mysterious and obscure, and doesn't have an obvious usefulness.

this object should be size small or smaller, and cannot be used to deal damage or cause conditions, it's not intended for combat utility.

think of why your character would adventure with it around, or at least, keep it close to them. Does it have sentimental value? or does it represent something about them?

### deities

= began writing comprehensive lore about the world. Starting to gain worldbuilding confidence.
- A lot of ideas here are recycled from Blue Divinity's lore.

There are no true deities in this world. There are false ones, and there are seats unfilled.

1. Nysyth: The pale angel: Nysyth is the deity of light. ageless, timeless, devoid all emotion, never fraught with fear or boiling with anger, and pristinely clear of love. His light is pure, as pure as Light can be. There should never be impurities within his domain. No colors, no darkness. He will sanitize worlds again and again until his impossible ideal of purity is met. 

2. Lanyin: the moonmaiden: a mortal, fair maiden, who ingested the elixir of immortality and rose to the heavenly palace. She now spends her lonely eternity in her decorated chariot, pulling the moon along the freezing cold night. She rests in the dark side when daybreak comes.

3. Morne: Morne is the deity of flowers. they have no title, no discernable form, and no voice. Morne exists whereever nature does, goes wherever the birds takes them, blooms where the sun graces the land, and offers sanctuary to all in their domain. Morne is the last of the old gods left.

4. Siet: The timekeeper. Siet is the deity of time and its endless march forward. He does not interfere with it, he simply comes to tidy what could disturb this delicate continuum. he is in all timelines, at all times, at once. Omniscient, yet not omnipotent.

5. There is no number 5.

6. Tsequ: A hollow, lifeless husk. he drifts across the dark beyond the celestial dome. He is the deity of the twilight, the border between day and night, between light and dark. he offers rest to those after day, and guards against the horrors of night. His governance continues. His ideals persist. Its unknown how a deity can be born dead. Its unknown if he even has power, or if his power is in the belief he has any.

### Locations
- started getting comfortable with writing long content blocks. The introduction of more and more worldbuilding was beginning to shape a real world to inhabit.
- intentionally tangled mysteries and oddies into them, prompting curiosity and hopefully, exploration in the campaign.

There are a few important locations to take note of. Reasonable to assume every PC knows.

1. Min. Min is a southern trade city, built on a convergence of Rivers. Built on an endless maze of canals, Min attracts poets and profit alike, their most prized cultural exports being drama, poetry, and art.

min is sheltered with a lattice of hanging gardens, of flowers that bloom in each season, and lanterns that rise into the sky at night. There is never a day without beauty in this city.

2. Thysqu: an isolated community of mystics far isolated in the southern mountains. Thysqu's elevation places them close to the borders between planes, but also closer to whatever lurks beyond the celestial dome.

thysqu's mystics often serve as enigmatic oracles and soothsayers for the royal family and the noble houses. Their readings are unerringly true. 

3. Ruhakh. An isolated, military bastion on the far north border of the empire. A sprawling maze of tall walls, taller towers, and endless soldiers. The city is constructed with black basalt, weathered from the bitter cold and the lack of care. 

Ruhakh is a prison - not for the most violent, but for the most careless. Political prisoners are sent here to starve. and so are innocent commoners, caught in the conflicts of the former.

4. Re nu: Re nu is the emperial great palace city, situated on a set of floating islands far above the peasants below. Great waterfalls descend from each side, as the city is suspended by a network of obsidian obelisks constructed from a bygone age.

Nobody knows who built the obelisks. or who suspended a landmass this high above the sky. The empire claims its a work of their greatest mages, but ask any of them, and you get the same response  - do they not know, or do they not want to know?

5. Lhosung: a city lost to time, buried under the desert that creeps from the southwest. Once a city of gold, a capital of a bygone desert dynasty, now reduced to nomadic tents along a trade route. 

however, Lhosung remains difficult to capture. The desert climate means land conquest is difficult, and the rogue local fighters sabotage imperial outposts. The locals fiercely guard their territory, and the precious gold that lays beneath the sand.

6. Jinhai: a community of distinguished scholars that grew into a small town. Built around a mysterious crater in the western border, with geometric patterns across the crash site, a lack of flora growth, and the most pressing mystery of all - how could something this big come down and make no disturbance? there were no reports of any loud sounds, any bright falling objects. No injuries are recorded. 

7. Yisu: a collection of islands far into the eastern sea, covered in maples with golden leaves year round. rarely do humans make the trecherous journey to reach this green paradise. 

there are ruins of a bygone civilisation. Ruins of great stone constructions and intricate sculpture. From what seem like ritual sites, community wells, noble housing - it was a full world, now left to the decay of time. researchers come and go, but each effort the empire makes to settle these islands leads to the mysterious dissapearance of the entire community.

Content being used:

it would complete a character’s mechanical side to their thematic identity.

Edition compatibility.
This campaign is compatible with 2014 or 2024 5e. The core campaign itself focuses on the world lore and its mysteries. It will run fine over any mechanical chassis.
Homebrew?
External homebrew material is not accepted. Internal homebrew - between players and DM - may be accepted with DM discretion. If internal homebrew can build more evocative, true to character PCs, whilst smoothing mechanical problems that may arise for this unusual premise, then it is welcome.

Campaign impressions (2/2):
Campaign and player style?
This is a campaign focused on Lore, intrigue, and exploration. Each player is called here through a mysterious reason, and the otherwise cosmic mysterious feel dangerously intimate.
This is a story heavy, narrative campaign. It will feature combat regularly, though it’s difficult to say how much or how often.
Campaign tone?
The campaign leans towards serious and gritty. It’s set in a darker version of a high fantasy world, but it would not be considered Grimdark.
It is not a campaign intended for those seeking a casual, simple premise or gameplay. It is expected that players know their character sheets thoroughly, and are willing to drive their personal stories forward. 
The DM?
The DM will provide the world, its inhabitants, its lore, and administrate it. The DM’s role is not to micromanage the game or the players. Instead, to give the players the right material and interesting story to let them drive the story forward and fill in the world. 
No such thing as main character
If you write a 20 page backstory, amazing!...but in a campaign with a setting like this, PCs are encouraged to explore the dread, anxiety, frustration, and hopelessness that comes with it. Your personal story will be dwarfed by the grand forces out of their control, some who wish to harm with vicious malevolence, and some do so anyway with indifference.
Yet the choice to succumb to these forces, is yours. That is a core part of the campaign. 
Once you’ve read all of the above, it’s time to start integrating your character into the world and its story.

From obsessive scholars to terrified common folk, there’s a call to action to dive into this mysterious mess of celestial and political intrigue.

Some suffer haunting nightmares of alternate worlds.

And many more…..
How that shard of forbidden knowledge found itself in their mind or body, is up to you. Could it be a secret pact? A curse passed down the bloodline? A psychic assault from an aberration? Or a tattoo pattern of a fanatic cult? Who knows? That’s up to you to decide.
Whatever it is that haunts your character’s core essence, the power they receive - it’s strange. Often volatile, often dangerous. Mechanically, this generally manifests as a warlock invocation.
eldritch origins
If we use 2014, which has no background feats, then each character is recommended to gain the eldritch adept feat specifically in place of a background feat. The campaign aims to involve each PC into the grand cosmic tapestry of the world, without shoving a specific background into their, you know, background. 
If we use 2024, you are free to take this feat instead of a generic origin feat.
However, if you feel a certain feat suits your character’s call to action better, there is room to discuss alternatives with the DM - especially if you already play a warlock. It’s important to remember whatever feat choice we give, it should fit the character’s mechanical identity, and is meant to focus more towards their non combat options.

Problematic spells…
D&D is a game filled to the brim with spells, but some unintentionally trivialise a lot of what makes this campaign fun and immersive. The below list will highlight explicitly banned character spell options, and what has been reinterpreted as RAI for this specific setting. Don’t worry, it’s not a long list.
And, these can still be found through magic items or spell scrolls as loot. It’s just you won’t be able to take them as prepared spells.
(**) Banned spells in general
Goodberry. Some sequences in the world will be gritty, and rely on managing hunger and injury. Goodberry trivialises this.
Purify / create food and drink. Same reason.
That’s it! Nothing else as of right now is explicitly banned. If a problem arises, we’ll let it pass for the session, and discuss how to tackle it for the next.
RAI - restricted or reinterpreted:
Comprehend languages / identify: yes, they tell you what it is, but they cannot provide the context behind it.
Create / destroy water. This water is not clean drinking water.
Small adjustments
Silvery barbs is now a 3rd level spell, if you wish to take it.

Combat
There is going to be combat, surprise surprise. How much or little is hard to say say, but it’s always a potential outcome.
For combat to work smoothly over online DnD, there are a few house rules to speed things up:
Pre roll initiative. So we can launch right into the action when it begins.
Plan your action. Be ready to act when it’s your turn, to keep the momentum going.
Diagonal movement. Moving diagonally will consume movement in a 1ft-2ft-1ft rhythm across squares.
Lunge / reach
If you are within ≤5ft short of distance to do something that you can reasonably reach, burn a bonus action to close that gap, instead of wasting a full action to dash. 
Opportunity Attacks: moving this extra ≤5 feet still provokes opportunity attacks as normal. It’s a physical stretch or lunging step. This is not the reach property for your weapons.
Bonus content (?):
Whilst a lot of the above has been focused on restrictions and balancing changes to ensure a balanced, pleasant experience going into this campaign, some more options are available as general QOL or setting specific additions:
Dunamancy spells
Whilst not available for your characters to pick directly to put on their spell list, Dunamancy spells will see an appearance throughout the game to represent the warping of space and time. 
Expanded feat list
If you find a feat that catches your eye, that isn’t part of the common faerun list of feats, we can of course take a look together at seeing if it will be allowed.
2024 exclusive: subclass spells
If your subclass frontloads you six different spells at level 3, I will allow you to access the cantrips and level 1 spells, at our starting level, if that’s what the original subclass would let you do, just because we’re not starting from level 3. However, if a subclass was already intended to be chosen at level 3, then this exception is void.
